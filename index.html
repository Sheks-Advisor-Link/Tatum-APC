Task (a) — Review of Draft Journal Entries (Document A)

1. Framework and preliminary classification

The performance incentive scheme is a share-based payment governed by IFRS 2, with deferred tax consequences per IAS 12. Two conceptual matters must be resolved before reviewing each journal.

(i) Nature of the vesting condition (CAGR in NAV per A-Ordinary share)

ViewTreatmentEffect
A — Non-market performance condition
Majority view for unlisted entities — NAV is an internally measured metric not a traded market price (IFRS 2 IG, Example 2).		 Condition affects vesting estimate (number of shares expected to vest), not grant-date fair value. Expense reversed if target not met. Bradger's implicit approach — consistent with straight-line recognition in FY2024/FY2025.
B — Market condition
NAV per share is arguably the "value of the entity's equity instruments" (IFRS 2 Appendix A definition).		 Condition factored into grant-date FV. Expense recognised regardless of whether target is met (provided service condition met). Less commonly applied — fair value is already low (R1,14) to reflect probability of meeting target.

View A is applied below (consistent with Bradger's own approach and SAICA exam convention), flagged for discussion with Auditco.

(ii) Classification of Options 1 and 2

  • Option 1 = Modification (not cancellation + new grant). IFRS 2.B43 — if a cancellation is accompanied by a replacement award identified as such, the combined transaction is accounted for as a modification. Bradger has incorrectly treated Option 1 as cancellation + new grant, materially distorting FY2025 and FY2026.
  • Option 2 = Cancellation with cash settlement (IFRS 2.28) — correctly identified by Bradger.

Correct. Straight-line allocation of original grant:

"The entity shall recognise, as a minimum, the services received measured at the grant-date fair value of the equity instruments granted" — IFRS 2.27

The original R1,14 per share continues to be expensed over the original 36-month vesting period. No acceleration applies.

Bradger has expensed R300 000 as an "excess". This is wrong. Per IFRS 2.28(c):

"Any payment made to the employee on the cancellation … shall be accounted for as the repurchase of an equity interest, i.e. as a deduction from equity, except to the extent that the payment exceeds the fair value of the equity instruments granted, measured at the repurchase date. Any such excess shall be recognised as an expense."

The relevant comparison is payment vs fair value at repurchase date, not payment vs SBP reserve balance.

3. Deferred tax — response to your specific query

Bradger is incorrect in omitting deferred tax. Deferred tax consequences arise for both options.

Option 1 (equity-settled modification)

Under section 11(lA) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 ("ITA"), the employer may deduct the market value of the shares issued to employees under a qualifying scheme, but only when the shares accrue to the employee (i.e. on vesting). The IFRS 2 expense is therefore not currently deductible.

Per IAS 12.68A–68C:

  • Tax base of services received to date = nil.
  • Estimated future tax deduction based on share price at reporting date (NAV ≈ R1,83 × 9m shares = R16 470 000).
  • DTA = min(cumulative expense, estimated deduction × proportion vested) × 27% — recognised in P/L.
  • Excess of estimated deduction over cumulative expense → DTA recognised in equity (IAS 12.68C).

Option 2 (cash-settled on cancellation)

  • R6m is cash remuneration, deductible in the year of payment (FY2026) under s11(a) and s7B of the ITA (variable remuneration deemed to accrue/be paid on actual payment).
  • In FY2025: R6m liability recognised for accounting; tax base = nil → DTA = R6m × 27% = R1 620 000.

Illustrative journal — Option 2, FY2025

Dr  Deferred tax asset (SFP)    R1 620 000
  Cr  Deferred tax (P/L)                   R1 620 000
Also consider: Employees' Tax (PAYE) under the Fourth Schedule to the ITA — the R6m cash payment is remuneration and PAYE must be withheld by Clairmont on payment.

  • BEE consequences: JZAH's voting control via B-Ordinary shares (10 votes each) is preserved (A-share issue dilutes dividend but not voting).
  • King IV: Conflicts of interest — Zeriah and Ayola are beneficiaries and directors; independent non-executive directors (Dr Reekie, Basil Oliphant, Lindiwe Smith) should approve.

Task (b) — Villa Flawty's Plan to Target Gauteng Government Departments (Document B)

Recommendation: The Board should NOT support the plan in its current form. Multiple actions conflict with the five procurement pillars and South African public-sector legislation, exposing Villa Flawty (and the CEOs as directors) to prosecution, blacklisting and reputational damage.

Assessment against the 5 procurement pillars and SA legislation

High risk Action 1 — Pre-tender site visits, experiential showcases and pre-tender event-planning guidance

Pillars breached: Open & Effective Competition; Ethics & Fair Dealing.

Legislative breaches:

  • PFMA s51/s57 (fiduciary duties of officials)
  • Treasury Regulation 16A; s217 of the Constitution (fair, equitable, transparent, competitive and cost-effective)
  • PRECCA, Act 12 of 2004, s3 and s4 (general & official corruption)
  • Public Service Regulations, 2016 — gift registers and declarations

Risk: Disqualification from bids; criminal prosecution; listing on the National Treasury's Register for Tender Defaulters (PRECCA s28); reputational harm.

High risk Action 2 — Complimentary suite upgrades and priority concierge for senior departmental representatives

Pillars breached: Ethics & Fair Dealing; Equity.

Legislative breaches:

  • PRECCA s3, s4, s12, s13 — offering "gratification" to public officers is an offence.
  • Public Administration Management Act; Code of Conduct for Public Servants (DPSA) — officials may not accept gifts > R350 without disclosure.

Risk: Criminal liability for both VF and the recipient officials. Directors of VF are personally liable under PRECCA s34 (duty to report corrupt activity).

High risk Action 3 — Managing scope changes through "direct stakeholder engagement" without written amendments

Pillar breached: Accountability & Reporting.

Legislative breaches:

  • PFMA s38(1)(a)(iii) requires proper record-keeping.
  • Treasury Regulation 16A6.4 requires contracts and variations to be in writing.
  • National Treasury SCM Instruction 03 of 2021/22 on contract management.

Risk: VF cannot enforce payment for unapproved variations; irregular expenditure may be disallowed by the Auditor-General, resulting in unpaid invoices. Potential fraud allegations.

High risk Action 4 — Standardised luxury hospitality package with consolidated (opaque) pricing

Pillars breached: Value for Money; Open Competition.

Legislative breaches:

  • Constitution s217.
  • Cost Containment Regulations, 2019 (GN 1081 of 14 October 2019) — restrictions on venues, entertainment and catering costs.
  • National Treasury Instruction Note on Cost Containment (2016, as updated).

Risk: Bid disqualification; inability to demonstrate market-related pricing per Treasury Regulation 16A6.3(a)(iii).

High risk Action 5 — Standardised extended payment terms to subcontractors (including SMMEs)

Pillars breached: Equity; Accountability.

Legislative breaches:

  • PFMA s38(1)(f) and Treasury Regulation 8.2.3 — government must pay within 30 days (the intent cascades to suppliers).
  • Preferential Procurement Regulations, 2022 (published 4 November 2022) — SMME promotion.
  • Companies Act s22 — reckless trading if suppliers are used as a credit line.

Risk: Loss of B-BBEE ESD score; reputational harm; possible Competition Commission scrutiny for abuse of buyer power.

  • Replace Action 1 with compliant market engagement — published RFIs, departmental industry days and open tender information sessions.
  • Remove all complimentary upgrades/gifts to officials. Offer uniform service levels.
  • Require written contract variations with approval trails.
  • Provide itemised, transparent pricing (per room, per delegate, per meal) enabling government to demonstrate VfM.
  • Align subcontractor payment cycles with VF's revenue cycle and pay SMMEs within 30 days — protecting B-BBEE ESD score and aligning with Treasury Regulation 8.2.3.

Task (c) — Strategic Options (Document C)

Option 1 — Joint Venture with Silver Spoons

Structuring / Legal
  • Legal form (partnership, joint operation, incorporated JV) — each has different Companies Act and IFRS 11 consequences. Incorporated JV is a separate entity.
  • Companies Act s112 — disposal of greater part of assets may require shareholder special resolution.
  • Competition Act 89 of 1998, s11–12 — merger notification if combined turnover/assets meet thresholds (R600m combined / R100m target).
  • Income Tax — contribution of kitchen/equipment at no charge may trigger s8(4)(a) recoupment and CGT unless corporate-rule relief (s42/s44) applies.
  • VAT Act s18 — use of VF's kitchen for no consideration may trigger deemed supply / change-of-use adjustments.
Operational / HR
  • Head chef retrenchment — LRA s189/189A consultation obligations; severance under BCEA s41 (1 week per year). Possible CCMA dispute.
  • Retention of other staff under Silver Spoons management may constitute a transfer of a going concern (s197 LRA) — automatic transfer on no-less-favourable terms.
  • Liquor Licence under Gauteng Liquor Act, 2003 — variation may be required if Silver Spoons becomes a "person having an interest".
Commercial / Financial
  • Loss of 100% of F&B EBITDA (2025: R1,77m); 50% share of JV profits dilutive unless Silver Spoons brand drives material uplift.
  • Facilitation fee to a related party — IAS 24 disclosure and potential Companies Act s75 personal financial interest.
  • Brand risk — F&B under Silver Spoons brand disassociates from VF's "afro-chic luxury" identity.
  • Erosion of bundled hospitality offering (harder to sell conference + catering).
Accounting (IFRS)
  • Joint control assessment. Joint venture → equity method (IAS 28). Joint operation → share of assets, liabilities, revenue, expense.
  • Removal of F&B line items from VF income statement; reclassified as share of profit of JV.

Option 2 — Internal Corporate Contracting Strategy

Operational
  • New dedicated account managers — fixed-cost increase.
  • CRM/systems investment for key account management.
  • Occupancy already 70% (11 680 × 70% ≈ 8 176 rooms sold) — limited weekday headroom and displacement risk of transient guests.
  • Upgrades / flexible booking = IFRS 15 variable consideration; constraint on revenue and possible deferred revenue for loyalty-type benefits (material rights).
Commercial
  • Discounted rates reduce ARR (currently R2 950) — margin compression.
  • Customer concentration risk — dependency on few corporates (IFRS 9 ECL on receivables).
  • Minimum volume commitments expose VF to shortfall risk.
  • Economic-downturn sensitivity of corporate travel budgets.
Strategic fit
  • Internally driven → lower execution risk, reversible if unsuccessful.
  • Retains full brand control and value chain — aligns with luxury positioning.
  • Does not address F&B underperformance flagged in Document 5.

For Option 2

  1. Financial projections (3-year P&L, cashflow, sensitivity) — explicitly absent.
  2. Current corporate client pipeline and win-rate assumptions.
  3. Competitor benchmarking in Sandton.
  4. Displacement analysis.
  5. Discount depth ranges and break-even occupancy uplift.
  6. Minimum booking volumes and contractual remedies.
  7. Conference facility current utilisation.
  8. Cost of establishing key-account-management function.
  9. Impact on luxury brand positioning.
  10. Credit-risk framework and receivables terms.
  11. B-BBEE scorecard impact.
  12. Exit flexibility and associated cost.

Summary for the Board

Option 1 is a transformational, higher-risk, externally dependent strategy that surrenders brand and operational control over F&B in exchange for potential demand uplift, while introducing related-party, labour, tax and competition-law complexity.
Option 2 is an incremental, internally controlled strategy with lower execution risk but narrower upside, material displacement risk at already-high occupancy, and no formal projections.
Neither option should be selected without the additional information listed. The options are not mutually exclusive — Option 2 could stabilise performance while Option 1 is fully scoped.
